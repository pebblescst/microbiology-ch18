Chapter Overview & Clinical Significance

Chapter 18 examines infections of the integumentary system (skin) and the deeper internal tissues — breast, fascia, tendons, lymphatic structures, bone, and joints. Because the skin is the body's first line of defense and the site of every surgical incision, this chapter is directly tied to the surgical technologist's role in preventing surgical site infections (SSIs).

CST Clinical Significance — Suture Closure & Dead Space (1 of 2) Suture closure of a laparotomy incision is performed in layers — peritoneum, then fascia and muscle as a unit, then the subcutaneous layer, then skin. The subcutaneous layer is fatty tissue resembling chicken fat with small blood vessels. Although this fat layer adds no strength to the closure, it is an ideal place for bacteria to multiply if pockets of space (dead space) are left between the two sides of the incision. Serosanguinous fluid that seeps from incised tissue postoperatively accumulates in dead space and serves as the perfect culture medium for bacteria — resulting in a surgical site infection (SSI).
CST Clinical Significance — Antimicrobial “Plus” Sutures (2 of 2) Suture material can become contaminated by indigenous or transient bacteria including S. aureus, S. epidermidis, MRSA, MRSE, E. coli, and K. pneumoniae. Wound infections can double the risk of death from complications. Ethicon's absorbable “Plus” sutures — Vicryl®, Monocryl®, PDS®, and barbed Stratafix™ — are manufactured with an antimicrobial coating of Triclosan that reduces the potential for wound layer infections by 28% based on Level 1-A clinical meta-analysis data. The WHO and CDC both recommend antimicrobial sutures as effective practice, and in 2021 the UK's National Health Service adopted Plus sutures as a best practice for reducing patient harm and the cost of treating SSIs.

The Integumentary System at a Glance

The human integumentary system — the largest organ of the body — comprises the skin, hair, nails, sweat glands, sebaceous glands, and sensory receptors. It is the first line of defense against infection, harmful radiation, and environmental irritants.

The Integumentary System & Skin Microbiota

The skin's structure and its resident microbial population work together to provide the first line of defense against infection.

Layers of the Skin

Epidermis

A layer of stratified epithelial tissue, further divided into sublayers (strata). Skin is waterproof but helps regulate internal body temperature by releasing water.

Dermis

Dense connective tissue found just beneath the epidermis. It connects the skin to the tissues below (adipose and muscle) and contains nerves and nourishing blood vessels. Together, the epidermis and dermis make up the cutaneous tissues.

Subcutaneous Layer

The adipose (fatty) layer beneath the cutaneous tissue. This is the “dead space” layer referenced in the chapter's clinical significance discussion — a prime location for bacterial proliferation if surgical closure leaves gaps.

Pathways for Pathogens The hair follicles, oil gland ducts, and sweat gland ducts of the dermis provide passageways for potential pathogens to make their way past the protective skin barrier and infect deeper body tissues. The normal microbiome of the skin contains many resident bacterial species that can penetrate this barrier as a result of traumatic injury or a planned surgical incision. Surgical technologists and other sterile team members are educated in how to prevent SSIs and recognize the bacterial species most often involved.

Indigenous Microbiota (Normal Flora) of the Skin

The indigenous microbiota, or microbiome, of the skin is also called the resident microbes or normal flora. Growth of the microbial population is influenced by moisture, pH, and temperature. Successful commensals thrive in high-salt environments and resist drying.

How Resident Flora Protect the Host

  • Crowd out more pathogenic species by occupying space in pores and on skin surfaces.
  • Several species produce substances that are toxic to other bacteria, preventing colonization by more pathogenic competitors.

Transient Microbes

Transient microbes are acquired through interaction with the surrounding environment and can include almost any type of microbe. They remain on the skin surface only until washed off, wiped off, or shed with normal epidermal cell turnover.

Bacterial Skin Infections

The microbiota of the skin is so abundant that skin infections are relatively common, even though skin has its own defense mechanisms. Wound infection occurs when the skin is broken and opportunistic microbes make their way into deeper layers — once past the barrier, they may enter lymph or blood and potentially cause septicemia. Skin lesions do not necessarily indicate infection, but they may signal a more serious systemic infection.

Staphylococcal Skin Infections
  • Staphylococcus aureus is the most common staphylococcal microbe and the most pathogenic of skin flora.
  • Staphylococci are responsible for boils, abscesses, and superficial folliculitis. S. aureus is a serious challenge for hospitals because it is carried and easily transmitted by patients, employees, and visitors.
  • Untreated infections can lead to tissue necrosis or cellulitis. Antibiotic-resistant strains are common because the organism is exposed to so many different antibiotics.
  • S. aureus is present in large numbers in nasal passages and is aerosolized after a sneeze or spread to the hands after touching the nose.
  • S. aureus can attach to hair follicles, causing folliculitis, which often appears as a pimple — if it occurs in an eyelash follicle, it causes a sty.
  • A badly infected follicle can form into a furuncle (boil) — a large, raised, pus-filled lesion surrounded by inflamed tissue, often treated with incision and drainage (I&D) and possible debridement of inflamed tissue.
  • Furuncles, carbuncles, and boils can be severe and may also be seen with other dermatologic infections such as cutaneous anthrax.
  • S. aureus and Streptococcus pyogenes are causative agents of impetigo, a skin disorder of thin-walled vesicles that rupture and crust over. If bacteria penetrate deep into the dermis, a crater-like ulcer called ecthyma can form.
Streptococcal Skin Infections
  • Streptococcus pyogenes (group A β-hemolytic streptococci) causes many diseases, including toxic shock syndrome, necrotizing fasciitis, streptococcal pharyngitis, and impetigo.
  • It also causes erysipelas, a serious dermal disease that manifests as red patches — typically on the face but possibly on other skin surfaces — usually preceded by streptococcal pharyngitis.
  • Colonies secrete toxins called hemolysins; species are characterized by the type produced (α-hemolytic and β-hemolytic). β-hemolytic streptococci are virulent pathogens whose toxins lyse red blood cells (erythrocytes).
  • Streptococci confined to the skin are typically easy to treat, but organisms that reach deeper tissues cause serious destruction through:
    • Streptokinases — enzymes that dissolve blood clots
    • Hyaluronidase — dissolves hyaluronic acid that cements connective tissue
    • Deoxyribonucleases — degrade DNA
  • Necrotizing fasciitis (“flesh-eating bacteria”) is a relatively rare disease resulting from invasion by group A streptococci. Bacterial enzymes destroy muscle and its fascial covering, and the toxin exotoxin A triggers the immune system to attack the area as an antigen, contributing to localized damage.
Pyoderma Gangrenosum (Necrotizing Fasciitis Mimic)
  • A rare skin condition often mistaken for necrotizing fasciitis.
  • An uninfected, non-contagious, ulcerative skin condition — an immunologic disorder associated with systemic disease and precipitated by trauma, but mistaken for an SSI when it occurs postoperatively.
  • Debridement may worsen skin destruction if the correct diagnosis is not made and appropriate non-surgical, immunosuppressive therapy is not started.
  • The non-infected ulcerated areas may later become colonized with opportunistic Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
Pseudomonas Infections of the Skin
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa is found in great numbers in hot tubs with inadequate chlorine levels. The EPA has set strict standards for public hot tubs/whirlpools in response to outbreaks of folliculitis — an infection of the hair follicles resulting in a rash.
  • The rash can be localized or widespread and can develop into severe ecthyma gangrenosum within 24 hours in immunocompromised individuals.
  • P. aeruginosa is an opportunistic species that causes infection in patients with severe burns. It is nearly impossible to prevent infection in burn patients, so the goal is to keep bacterial cell counts as low as possible to prevent septicemia.
  • Topical antibiotic creams have limited control over colonization by P. aeruginosa. Burn patients undergo frequent surgical debridement of necrotic skin to help control infection.
  • P. aeruginosa grows easily in dilute disinfectants, so hospital personnel must take special precautions. More pseudomonad species are becoming antibiotic-resistant.
Acne Infections
  • Acne is a bacterial skin disease characterized by inflammatory pustules, cysts, and papules caused by S. aureus, Propionibacterium acnes, and Corynebacterium.
  • Most teenagers experience some form of the disease, suggesting it can be triggered by fluctuating hormones during puberty. Teens and young adults may suffer a severe form called cystic acne, which leaves scars on the face and upper body.
  • Infection is caused by blocked channels designed for the passage of sebum to the skin surface. Sebum accumulates and forms whiteheads; as the top of the whitehead is exposed to air, it becomes a blackhead.
  • P. acnes metabolizes sebum and forms free fatty acids that lead to inflammation. White blood cells and interstitial fluid accumulate and create cysts that rupture and cause scarring.
  • Acne vulgaris is a severe form of acne caused by P. acnes. Topical creams of varying strength and oral antibiotics are used to treat more serious acne infections.
Hansen's Disease (Leprosy)
  • More commonly known as leprosy; once a highly feared disease dating back centuries, caused by the acid-fast Mycobacterium leprae, an obligate intracellular bacillus.
  • The pathogen may be found in macrophages, Schwann cells, muscle cells, and endothelial cells, and presents in two forms:
    • Neural (tuberculoid) form — results in erythematous skin macules and peripheral nerve lesions.
    • Cutaneous (lepromatous) form — results in progressive, disfiguring skin nodules, papules, and macules occurring throughout the body.
Cutaneous Anthrax
  • Bacillus anthracis, the bacterium that causes anthrax, takes four forms: cutaneous, inhalation, gastrointestinal, and injection.
  • Cutaneous anthrax is the least dangerous and most common form, although all forms are potentially life-threatening systemic infections. Transmission occurs through cuts or scrapes during contact with hides, hair, or wool of infected animals.
  • Small bumps or blisters appear 1 day to 2 months after exposure. When blisters fade, a lesion with a necrotic black center remains and eventually sloughs and heals with proper treatment.
Gas Gangrene (Myonecrosis)
  • A serious infection of skin or postoperative surgical wounds caused most commonly by Clostridium perfringens; group A Streptococcus, Staphylococcus aureus, and Vibrio vulnificus are other possible pathogens.
  • Patients with atherosclerosis, diabetes, or colon cancer are at greater risk for gas gangrene infections.
  • Toxins released by C. perfringens produce painful swelling and subcutaneous emphysema (air under the skin), which creates crepitus when pressed.
  • Tissue necrosis is extremely rapid, so immediate IV antibiotics and debridement — or amputation of the extremity — are crucial for survival. Hyperbaric oxygen may subsequently be used for wound healing.

Fungal Skin Infections

Many fungi cause cutaneous mycoses, which may invade skin, hair, and nails, growing as filaments (hyphae). Fungi are typical aerobes that affect only the outer, dead layer of skin and cannot grow into deeper regions, but they can secrete toxic substances that diffuse into the dermal regions, causing redness.

Cutaneous Mycoses (Dermatomycoses)

Superficial fungal infections are called dermatomycoses and are easily spread. Each scale of an infected area contains hyphae and spores that can grow readily if passed from one individual to another — indirect transmission can occur in shower stalls or through shared personal toiletries. Pathogens responsible for dermatomycoses are species of Microsporum, Epidermophyton, and Trichophyton; treatment is often topical antifungals.

Subcutaneous Mycoses

Sporotrichosis — “Gardener's Disease” Subcutaneous mycoses are more serious than superficial cutaneous infections. Soil-dwelling fungi can penetrate skin layers and enter deeper areas. The most common form, sporotrichosis, is caused by Sporothrix schenckii. It infects gardeners who do not wear gloves — the fungus enters through an opening in the hand and gets into the lymphatic and circulatory systems, where it travels to different areas and causes potentially serious lesions.

Candidiasis

Candida albicans

Candidiasis is a fungal infection caused by Candida albicans, a yeast-like resident microflora of the mouth and GI tract. Conditions including immunocompromise, diabetes, chronic steroid or antibiotic therapy, and pregnancy can cause the fungus to proliferate and change its growth form.

Systemic Mycoses

The Most Serious Fungal Infections

Those infected most likely inhaled spores living as saprophytes in the soil. The spores can lodge and grow in lung tissue, potentially causing respiratory difficulty. From the lung, fungi can spread through lymph and blood to other organ systems.

Viral Skin Infections

Viral skin diseases range from minor (warts) to major (smallpox) infections. The term “pox” is Latin for “spotted” and is used for any skin infection manifested by a serious rash or pustule formation.

Warts (Papillomas)
  • Warts, or papillomas, are caused by more than 50 types of papillomaviruses (HPV).
  • They may be removed in a physician's office with liquid nitrogen spray to freeze them off.
Chickenpox (Varicella Zoster Virus)
  • Chickenpox is a respiratory infection caused by varicella zoster virus (VZV).
  • Characterized by formation of vesicles on the skin or mucous membranes of the face, thorax, and neck. The vesicles fill with pus, rupture, and form scabs.
  • In rare cases, VZV can cause encephalitis or pneumonia.
Shingles (Herpes Zoster)
  • VZV remains latent in the body for a lifetime after the initial infection and moves to the peripheral nerves, ending up in the dorsal root ganglion near the spine.
  • The virus may be reactivated by stress or aging and move into superficial sensory nerves, resulting in a painful condition known as shingles (herpes zoster).
  • Shingles is a different form of chickenpox — less severe due to partial immunity from the initial infection. Lesions appear as in chickenpox but are more localized, typically on only one side of the body. Because the virus exists in the nerve ganglion and manifests in cutaneous nerves, it is more painful than chickenpox.
  • Management includes antiviral medication; corticosteroids may relieve inflammation, and prescription pain relievers or low-dose antidepressants may ease the debilitating pain, also called post-herpetic neuralgia. In exceptionally painful outbreaks, a nerve block may be performed.
Shingrix Vaccine Shingles can be prevented with a vaccine given prior to an outbreak. The CDC recommends that adults age 50 and older receive the Shingrix recombinant zoster vaccine — even if they have previously had shingles, are unsure whether they had chickenpox as a child, or previously received the Zostavax vaccine. Shingrix provides 90% protection initially and continues to provide nearly 85% protection for at least four years.
Smallpox (Variola Virus)
  • Smallpox is caused by the variola virus in two forms: variola major (mortality rate of 30% or higher) and variola minor (mortality rate of less than 1%).
  • Variola major has four types:
    • Ordinary — the most common form (90%)
    • Modified — a mild form seen in previously vaccinated individuals
    • Flat and hemorrhagic — both very rare but usually fatal illnesses
  • Transmission occurs through respiratory aerosols and the lungs. The variola virus infects multiple internal organs before the skin. The recognizable skin lesions that form are highly disfiguring.
Smallpox Vaccine & Vaccinia Reactions
  • The CDC maintains laboratory samples of the variola virus for clinical study; samples may also be maintained in other countries. Smallpox has potential for use as a biological weapon, so research stockpiles are closely guarded and protected.
  • The CDC states there are currently adequate supplies of smallpox vaccine for every person in the U.S. in case of a bioterrorism incident.
  • Live vaccinia virus — not smallpox — is used for the vaccine and produces a vigorous immune response without possible smallpox infection. A bifurcated (two-pronged) needle, not a hypodermic needle, is dipped into live vaccinia vaccine and used to prick the upper arm of the recipient multiple times.
  • Progressive vaccinia (also called vaccinia necrosum) is an ongoing infection of skin with tissue destruction caused by vaccination in persons with inadequate immune responses. This statistically rare post-vaccination infection progresses rapidly and is frequently fatal. A small number (14–52) out of every 1 million persons vaccinated in the past suffered devastating reactions.

Infections of Internal Tissues

Internal tissues — the breast, fascia, tendon sheaths, bone, joints, and lymph nodes — are susceptible to infection by many of the same pathogens as other body systems. Infections involving the skin may extend beyond a localized area and penetrate into internal tissues normally free from bacteria.

Breast Tissue Infections (Mastitis)
  • Mastitis refers to generalized or localized infection/inflammation of breast tissue, typically associated with breastfeeding or pregnancy.
  • Lactational mastitis — uncommon, occurs in lactating females, often when nipples become cracked from breastfeeding. Bacteria (often S. aureus or MRSA) from the infant's mouth invade milk ducts, causing inflammation, potential blockage, and pain in the breast.
  • Chronic subareolar abscess — occurs if sebaceous glands around the nipples become infected; pus and serous fluid from the inflammatory response create an edematous area.
  • Non-lactational mastitis — similar to lactational mastitis but usually localized, and may follow a primary skin infection or cellulitis. Females who have had a lumpectomy or partial mastectomy followed by radiation therapy may develop this form. Females with diabetes or a compromised immune system may also be at risk.
Fasciitis & Tendonitis
  • Infections of the fascia or tendons — the sheets or sheaths of fibrous connective tissue that line muscles or tendons.
  • Infections may occur when skin penetration into the dermis allows opportunistic pathogens to infiltrate deeper fascial layers.
  • Necrotizing fasciitis — a fulminating group A streptococcal infection that begins as extensive cellulitis and spreads to the superficial and deep fascia.
  • Bacterial tendonitis is uncommon, involves the tendon sheath, and is not related to inflammatory repetitive movement or acute strain. Tubular tendon sheaths are lined with synovial membrane for lubrication and enclose tendons. Tenosynovitis results from direct bacterial contamination of a traumatic wound that breaks the skin and could result in necrosis.
  • Symptoms include pain on extension, tenderness, edema, or lymphadenopathy (lymph node swelling).
Lymphadenitis & Lymphangitis
  • Lymphadenitis — inflammation and possible visible/palpable enlargement of one or more lymph nodes as a result of benign, localized, or generalized infections.
    • Generalized lymph node enlargement is common in infectious mononucleosis, cytomegalovirus infection, brucellosis, secondary syphilis, and disseminated histoplasmosis.
    • Regional lymph node enlargement is prominent in streptococcal infection, tuberculosis, primary syphilis, plague, chancroid, and genital herpes simplex.
  • Lymphadenitis may be acute, subacute, or chronic. Node enlargement is due to proliferation of cells intrinsic to the node (lymphocytes, plasma cells, monocytes, or histiocytes). Viruses, bacteria, protozoa, rickettsiae, or fungi can all cause lymphadenitis.
  • Lymphangitis — inflammation of lymphatic vessels that transport lymph fluid; a sign that pathogens have invaded the blood and lymphatic systems and are circulating (septicemia). It causes red streaks under the skin that run the length of an extremity away from the original source of infection, toward the heart.
Osteomyelitis
  • Acute or chronic infection of the bone that may result in destruction of bone and spread to joints, causing stiffness and pain.
  • Acute osteomyelitis is typically the result of invasion by Staphylococcus aureus; Streptococcus pyogenes and Haemophilus influenzae are less frequent causes. It affects the epiphyseal plates of long bones due to decreased blood flow that allows bacteria in the bloodstream to settle and grow. It is most often seen in rapidly growing children and adolescents and can result in extremity shortening.
  • Chronic osteomyelitis is usually caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis and is a secondary infection arising from a primary pulmonary infection. Salmonella, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Treponema pallidum may cause chronic bone infections and severe bone lesions in children with congenital syphilis.
Arthritis
  • An inflammatory disease of the joints causing pain, edema, and restricted motion. The two main forms are osteoarthritis and rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic systemic disease with inflammatory changes throughout the body's connective tissues, regarded as an autoimmune disease in which the body produces antibodies against its own cells and tissues.
  • Septic arthritis results from bacterial invasion of the joint from the bloodstream (bacteremia) or from direct inoculation of the joint through penetrating trauma. S. aureus is again the most common pathogen, although H. influenzae and Salmonella may cause septic arthritis in children.
  • These pathogens usually affect only a single joint, but rare infections caused by N. gonorrhoeae can affect many joints — a condition known as polyarthritis. Hip or knee joints may be infected by M. tuberculosis.
